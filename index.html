📐 What is SVD?

For any real matrix \( A \) of size \( m \times n \), the Singular Value Decomposition is:

\[ A = U \Sigma V^T \]
  • U (\(m \times m\)): Left singular vectors (orthonormal columns).
  • \(\Sigma\) (\(m \times n\)): Diagonal matrix with singular values \(\sigma_1 \ge \sigma_2 \ge ... \ge 0\).
  • V (\(n \times n\)): Right singular vectors (orthonormal columns).

🌀 Geometric Meaning of SVD Components

Any linear transformation \( \mathbf{x} \mapsto A\mathbf{x} \) can be decomposed as:

Rotation/Reflection (by \(V^T\)) → Scaling (by \(\Sigma\)) → Rotation (by \(U\)).

The singular values \(\sigma_i\) represent the lengths of the semi-axes of the ellipse (ellipsoid) that is the image of the unit sphere under \(A\).

🔵 Unit circle (input)  →  🟠 Transformed ellipse (output) - FULLY VISIBLE

\(A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}\)
\(\sigma_1 \approx 2.618, \sigma_2 \approx 0.382\) (ellipse axes scaled by σ)

🎯 Right singular vectors v₁, v₂ (blue thick) & Transformed vectors A·v₁, A·v₂ (orange dashed) — labels upright

Key Insight: The SVD reveals the intrinsic geometric action: rotate/stretch/rotate. The condition number = \(\sigma_{max}/\sigma_{min}\).

💡 Interpretation: Largest singular value = maximum gain, smallest = minimum gain. Columns of V are orthonormal basis that align with stretching axes.

📝 Step-by-Step Hand Calculation: 2×2 Matrix

Let \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \). Find SVD manually.

1️⃣ Compute \( A^T A \): \( \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \)
2️⃣ Eigenvalues of \(A^T A\): \(\lambda_1=9, \lambda_2=1\) → singular values: \(\sigma_1=3, \sigma_2=1\).
3️⃣ Right singular vectors (V): Normalized eigenvectors: \(v_1 = (1,0)^T, v_2=(0,1)^T\). So \(V = I\).
4️⃣ Left singular vectors (U): \(u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A v_i\) → \(u_1 = \frac{1}{3} A (1,0)^T = (1,0)^T\), \(u_2 = \frac{1}{1} A(0,1)^T = (0,1)^T\). So \(U = I\).
5️⃣ Verify: \(U \Sigma V^T = I \begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix} I = A\) ✅

Another classic: \( A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \)

✨ Compute \(A^T A = \begin{bmatrix}5&4\\4&5\end{bmatrix}\) → eigenvalues 9 and 1, \(\sigma_1=3,\sigma_2=1\).
Eigenvectors: \(v_1 = (\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^T\), \(v_2 = (-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^T\).
Then \(u_1 = \frac{1}{3}A v_1 = (\frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}/3, \frac{3}{\sqrt{2}}/3)^T = (\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^T\).
\(u_2 = A v_2 = (-\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}})^T\) (norm=1). Hence SVD: \(A = U \begin{bmatrix}3&0\\0&1\end{bmatrix} V^T\).

🚀 SVD in Machine Learning & Large Language Models

🧠 1. Dimensionality Reduction (PCA via SVD): Principal Component Analysis is performed by applying SVD to the centered data matrix.
🤖 2. LLM Weight Compression & LoRA: LoRA uses low-rank decomposition \(W = W_0 + BA\) reducing parameters by 10,000x.
🔍 3. Recommendation Systems: User-item matrix factorization.
📚 4. Word Embeddings & LSA: Reveals latent topics.
⚡ 5. Attention Approximation: Linformer uses SVD-like low-rank attention.
